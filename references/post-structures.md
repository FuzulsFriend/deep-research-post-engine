# Post Structures — 8 Proven Frameworks

> Reference document for the Deep Research Post Engine skill.
> Each framework includes: name, when to use, line-by-line template, full example, and why it works.

---

## 1. The Contrarian Take

**"Everyone says X. The data says Y."**

### When to Use
When your research uncovered surprising findings that challenge conventional wisdom. Best for topics where the audience has a strong default assumption you can shatter with evidence. Drives comments because people feel compelled to debate.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — Bold contrarian statement that challenges a popular belief]

[Line 2 — Blank line for visual breathing room]

[Lines 3-5 — "Here's what everyone tells you:" + the conventional wisdom in 2-3 short bullets]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — Pivot: "But here's what the data actually shows:"]

[Lines 9-14 — Your evidence in 3-4 numbered points. Each point: stat or finding + 1-sentence implication]

[Line 15 — Blank line]

[Lines 16-18 — The "so what": what this means for the reader's decisions or strategy]

[Line 19 — Blank line]

[Line 20 — Specific question that invites disagreement: "Where do you see this differently?"]
```

### Example Post

Stop optimizing your LinkedIn posts for likes. Likes are almost irrelevant to your reach.

Here's what most LinkedIn "experts" tell you:
- Post engagement-bait questions
- Use trending hashtags
- Thank people for likes in comments

Here's what the 2026 algorithm actually rewards:

1. Dwell time — posts people spend 60+ seconds reading get 13x more distribution than posts people scroll past
2. Saves — when someone bookmarks your post, LinkedIn treats it as the strongest quality signal
3. DM shares — private forwards indicate your content is valuable enough to recommend 1-on-1
4. Comment depth — a 3-reply thread is worth more than 50 "Great post!" comments

The creators getting massive reach in 2026 aren't chasing likes. They're writing content so useful that people save it, share it privately, and come back to reference it.

What signal are you actually optimizing for?

### Why It Works
Contrarian posts create cognitive dissonance — readers feel the tension between what they believed and what you're showing them, so they stop scrolling (high dwell time). The disagreement element drives comments as people either defend their existing view or share their own evidence. Data-backed contrarian takes generate the highest save rates because people want to reference the numbers later.

## 2. The Personal Experiment

**"I tested X for Y days. Here's what happened."**

### When to Use
When you or the user has first-hand experience running an experiment, trying a new tool, changing a process, or testing a hypothesis. The specificity of "I tested this myself" creates instant credibility. High dwell time because readers want to see the results before the end.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "I [did specific thing] for [time period]. The results surprised me."]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-5 — Context: Why you ran this experiment. What was the problem or question?]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — "Here's what I did:" — 2-3 sentences describing the methodology simply]

[Line 9 — Blank line]

[Lines 10-11 — "The results:"]

[Lines 12-18 — 3-5 numbered findings. Each: specific metric or observation + what it means. Lead with the most surprising finding.]

[Line 19 — Blank line]

[Lines 20-22 — The lesson: what you'd tell someone who's about to try the same thing]

[Line 23 — Blank line]

[Line 24 — Question: "Has anyone else tested this? What did you find?"]
```

### Example Post

I replaced all our team's status meetings with async Loom videos for 30 days. Here's what actually happened.

We had 12 recurring meetings per week across a team of 8. Total: 47 hours of meeting time weekly. I was convinced we could cut that in half.

So I ran the experiment: every status update became a 3-5 minute Loom video. Decisions still happened in live calls. Everything else was async.

The results after 30 days:

1. Meeting hours dropped from 47 to 11 per week — a 77% reduction
2. Project velocity actually increased by 23% (measured in completed tickets)
3. The team reported higher satisfaction — but 2 people said they felt more isolated
4. Context-switching interruptions dropped by 60%
5. One unexpected downside: conflict resolution took longer without face-to-face conversation

The biggest lesson: async works brilliantly for information transfer but poorly for relationship building. The winning formula was async for updates, live for decisions and conflict.

Has anyone else experimented with eliminating meetings? I'd love to compare notes.

### Why It Works
Experiment posts tap into the scientific curiosity loop — "what happened?" creates a powerful read-through impulse that maximizes dwell time. The specificity of real numbers (77%, 23%, 30 days) builds credibility and makes the post save-worthy. People who've had similar experiences are compelled to share their own results in comments, driving the conversation-depth signal LinkedIn rewards most.

## 3. The Data Reveal

**"I analyzed X things. Here are the surprising findings."**

### When to Use
When you have numbers, data, or patterns from analysis. The word "analyzed" signals authority and the word "surprising" creates curiosity tension. Gets saved because data = reference material. Numbers in the hook stop the scroll.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "I analyzed [specific number] [things]. [One surprising finding as a teaser]."]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-5 — Context: What you analyzed and why. Keep it to 2-3 sentences.]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — "Here's what I found:"]

[Lines 9-20 — 5-7 numbered findings. Structure each as: "[Finding]" + "[Implication/what this means]". Order from most to least surprising. Include specific percentages, counts, or comparisons.]

[Line 21 — Blank line]

[Lines 22-24 — Your interpretation: the one overarching takeaway that ties the findings together]

[Line 25 — Blank line]

[Line 26 — Save prompt: "Saving this for anyone who wants the full breakdown." OR a question: "Which finding surprises you most?"]
```

### Example Post

I analyzed 200 LinkedIn posts from tech founders over the past 3 months. The posts that went viral had almost nothing in common with what "best practices" recommend.

I tracked engagement rates, comment quality, and time-to-peak for each post. I was looking for what actually drives reach vs. what people think drives reach.

Here's what I found:

1. Posts without any hashtags outperformed posts with 5+ hashtags by 34%
2. The highest-performing posts were 1,400-1,800 characters — not short, punchy "hot takes"
3. Posts published between 4-6 PM got 2.1x more engagement than the "optimal" 10 AM slot
4. Only 3 out of 200 viral posts used engagement-bait questions at the end
5. 67% of the top performers shared original data or personal experience — zero were reshared articles
6. Posts that received their first comment from the author within 5 minutes averaged 3.8x more total engagement
7. The single strongest predictor of virality: number of saves within the first hour

The overarching pattern: LinkedIn in 2026 rewards depth and originality over formatting tricks. The algorithm has gotten remarkably good at distinguishing substance from performance.

Which finding surprises you most?

### Why It Works
Numbers in the hook are proven scroll-stoppers on LinkedIn — the brain processes them faster than words, creating an instant value assessment. Data reveals get saved at high rates because people treat them as reference material they'll cite in their own content. The list format maximizes dwell time as readers process each finding, and the "which surprises you most?" question drives specific (not generic) comments.

## 4. The Story Arc

**Hook > Struggle > Turning Point > Lesson**

### When to Use
When you have a genuine personal or professional story with a clear turning point. Emotional posts get shared via DM — the strongest signal in LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm. Best for building trust and connection with your audience. Storytelling posts generate the highest number of comments because they invite readers to share similar experiences.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — Hook: A vivid moment that drops the reader into the middle of the action. Present tense if possible.]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-7 — The struggle: Set the scene. What was the challenge, the failure, the difficult situation? Be specific — names, dates, details make it real. 3-5 sentences.]

[Line 8 — Blank line]

[Lines 9-13 — The turning point: What changed? A conversation, a realization, a piece of data, a decision. This is the pivot of the story. 3-5 sentences.]

[Line 14 — Blank line]

[Lines 15-19 — The lesson: What did you learn? Frame it as applicable to the reader, not just your situation. 2-4 sentences.]

[Line 20 — Blank line]

[Line 21 — Emotional close: a question that invites readers to share their own version of this story.]
```

### Example Post

"We're shutting down the project." My manager said it calmly, like she was ordering lunch. I'd spent 8 months building it.

In early 2024, I pitched a developer tool that I was convinced would change how our team shipped code. I got the green light, assembled a small team, and built exactly what I'd envisioned. Beautiful architecture. Clean code. Every feature I'd specced out.

The problem? In 8 months, not a single team outside ours adopted it. Zero. I'd built something technically excellent that solved a problem nobody actually had.

The turning point came in the shutdown meeting. My manager asked: "How many potential users did you talk to before you started building?" The answer was none. I'd validated the idea with myself.

That failure taught me the most important lesson of my career: the quality of your solution is irrelevant if you haven't validated the problem. Since then, I don't write a single line of code until I've had 10 conversations with potential users. Not surveys. Not Slack messages. Real conversations.

It's been two years since that shutdown. Every product I've built since has found users in week one.

What's a professional failure that changed how you work?

### Why It Works
Stories activate neural coupling — the reader's brain literally mirrors the emotional journey of the writer, creating deep engagement and maximum dwell time. The vulnerability of sharing failure triggers reciprocity; readers want to share their own stories in comments. DM sharing spikes on emotional posts because people send them to specific colleagues thinking "you need to read this" — which is LinkedIn's strongest distribution signal.

## 5. The How-To (Expert)

**Deep on ONE technique, not "5 tips"**

### When to Use
When you can teach something genuinely actionable — a specific technique, process, or method that the reader can implement immediately. Gets saved for later reference, which is one of LinkedIn's strongest engagement signals. The key differentiator from generic advice posts: go deep on ONE thing instead of shallow on five.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "Most people [do common approach]. Here's the technique that [specific better outcome]."]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-5 — The problem: Why the common approach fails. Be specific about the pain point.]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — "Here's the technique I use instead:"]

[Line 9 — Blank line]

[Lines 10-20 — Step-by-step breakdown of the ONE technique. 4-6 steps, each with:
  - Step name/action (bolded or numbered)
  - 1-2 sentences explaining HOW to do it
  - Optional: specific example or tool recommendation]

[Line 21 — Blank line]

[Lines 22-24 — The result: What happens when you apply this. Include a specific outcome if possible.]

[Line 25 — Blank line]

[Line 26 — "Save this for next time you [relevant situation]." OR "What's your approach to [topic]?"]
```

### Example Post

Most people write LinkedIn hooks by staring at a blank screen until something clever comes out. Here's the technique that generates 10 hooks in 5 minutes.

The problem with "inspiration-based" hook writing is that it's inconsistent. Some days you're sharp, some days you're not. Your posting consistency suffers because the first line is the hardest part.

Here's the technique I use instead:

Step 1: Start with the outcome, not the topic. Don't write about "productivity." Write the specific result: "I cut my work hours from 60 to 35 without losing output."

Step 2: Apply one of three tension patterns:
- Contrast: "Everyone does X. I did the opposite."
- Curiosity gap: "I [specific action]. [Unexpected result]."
- Specificity: "[Exact number] + [exact timeframe] + [exact outcome]."

Step 3: Remove every adjective. "Amazing results" becomes "2.3x output." Specifics beat superlatives.

Step 4: Read it as if you're scrolling LinkedIn at 8 AM with coffee. Would YOU stop? If not, apply more tension.

Step 5: Write 10 variations using all three tension patterns. Pick the one where you think "I need to know what comes next."

I've used this for every post in the last 6 months. My average dwell time went from 12 seconds to 48 seconds — a 4x improvement — because the right hook earns the read.

Save this for next time you're stuck on that first line.

### Why It Works
Expert how-to posts earn saves at the highest rate of any content type because they provide immediate, implementable value — people bookmark them as a reference tool. Going deep on ONE technique (instead of listicle-style tips) creates the perception of genuine expertise, which builds the author's authority signal with LinkedIn's algorithm. The step-by-step format keeps readers progressing through the post, driving strong dwell time.

## 6. The News Analysis

**Breaking development > What it means for [audience]**

### When to Use
When something just happened in your industry — a product launch, regulatory change, market shift, research paper, funding round, or company announcement. Timely content gets high impressions because people are searching for context. You must post fast — within hours, not days. The value is not reporting the news (people already know) but explaining what it means.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "[Specific thing] just happened. Here's what it means for [your audience]."]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-5 — The facts: What happened, stripped to the essentials. 2-3 sentences. No editorializing yet.]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — "Here's why this matters:"]

[Lines 9-15 — 3-4 implications, each with:
  - The implication (what changes)
  - Why it matters to the reader specifically
  - What they should do about it (if applicable)]

[Line 16 — Blank line]

[Lines 17-20 — Your unique take: What most coverage is missing. This is where your expertise shows. 2-3 sentences.]

[Line 21 — Blank line]

[Line 22 — "How do you see this playing out?" or "What's your read on this?"]
```

### Example Post

OpenAI just announced that GPT-5 is free for all developers under 1M tokens/month. Here's what this means if you're building AI products.

The facts: Starting April 1, any developer can access GPT-5 (their most capable model) via API with no upfront cost, up to 1 million tokens per month. After that, standard pricing applies. This was announced at their developer conference yesterday.

Here's why this matters:

1. The "AI wrapper" business model just got harder. If your product's only value is providing access to GPT-5, you now compete with free. Defensibility must come from data, workflow integration, or domain expertise — not model access.

2. The developer experimentation wave will be massive. Every side project, hackathon team, and early-stage startup can now prototype with the best model. Expect an explosion of AI tools in the next 90 days.

3. Enterprise buyers will demand more. When the underlying model is free, the conversation shifts entirely to ROI, security, compliance, and integration. If you sell to enterprise, retool your pitch now.

What most coverage misses: this is an acquisition strategy, not generosity. OpenAI is building the largest developer ecosystem in AI. Free GPT-5 today means paid GPT-6 dependency tomorrow. This is the AWS playbook from 2012.

How do you see this playing out for your team?

### Why It Works
News analysis posts ride the wave of existing attention — people are already searching for and discussing the topic, so your post enters a high-traffic conversation. The value-add is interpretation, not information, which positions you as a thinker, not a reporter. Timely posts earn high impression counts and profile visits (a strong algorithm signal), and the analysis format drives comments from people who want to add their own perspective.

## 7. The Before/After

**"This is what X looked like before. This is after."**

### When to Use
When you have a transformation to show — a process that improved, a metric that changed, a skill that developed, or a mindset that shifted. The visual/conceptual contrast creates high dwell time as readers process both states. Works especially well with carousel format but also effective in text.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "Here's what [thing] looked like [time period] ago. And here's what it looks like now."]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-8 — BEFORE: Describe the previous state in vivid detail. Use specific numbers, emotions, or situations. Make the reader feel the pain or limitation. 4-6 sentences.]

[Line 9 — Blank line]

[Lines 10-11 — The transition: What changed? Keep it to 1-2 sentences. The pivot should feel clean.]

[Line 12 — Blank line]

[Lines 13-18 — AFTER: Describe the current state with equal specificity. Mirror the structure of the BEFORE section so the contrast is sharp. Show concrete improvements. 4-6 sentences.]

[Line 19 — Blank line]

[Lines 20-23 — The insight: What drove the transformation? Not just "I worked hard" — identify the specific decision, tool, or mindset shift that made the difference. 2-3 sentences.]

[Line 24 — Blank line]

[Line 25 — "What transformation has surprised you most in your own work?"]
```

### Example Post

Here's what my morning routine looked like 2 years ago. And here's what it looks like now.

BEFORE: Wake up at 7:15 after hitting snooze 3 times. Grab phone immediately — scroll through 47 Slack messages, 12 emails, and 3 "urgent" requests before my feet hit the floor. Rush through getting ready. Arrive at my desk at 8:30 already feeling behind. Spend the first 2 hours in reactive mode, answering other people's priorities. By noon I hadn't started any deep work. My creative output was suffering and I couldn't figure out why.

Then I ran an experiment: no phone and no Slack until 10 AM. Just 2 hours of protected deep work every morning.

AFTER: Wake at 6:30, no alarm needed (turns out I wasn't tired, I was drained). First 2 hours: focused work on my highest-priority project. No Slack, no email, no notifications. I ship more meaningful work before 10 AM than I used to ship in an entire day. My quarterly output has roughly doubled. And the "urgent" messages from Slack? In 8 months, not a single one was actually urgent enough that 2 hours mattered.

The insight that changed everything: I wasn't bad at time management. I was letting other people's urgency dictate my priorities. The 2-hour morning block didn't just protect my time — it forced me to decide what actually mattered most before anyone else could decide for me.

What transformation has surprised you most in your own work?

### Why It Works
The before/after structure exploits the contrast effect — the human brain processes differences more powerfully than absolutes, so readers feel the magnitude of change viscerally. The mirrored structure (before details vs. after details) creates satisfying pattern completion that keeps readers engaged through the whole post. Transformation stories get shared via DM because people send them to specific friends thinking "you should try this," triggering LinkedIn's highest-weighted engagement signal.

## 8. The Unpopular Opinion

**"I know this will be controversial, but..."**

### When to Use
When you genuinely disagree with a consensus position in your industry and you can back it up with logic, experience, or data. The key word is "genuinely" — manufactured controversy gets detected and penalized. Only use this when you have a real, defensible position. The controversy drives comments, but the substance drives saves and shares.

### Structure Template

```
[Line 1 — "Unpopular opinion: [bold, clear statement of your position]"]

[Line 2 — Blank line]

[Lines 3-5 — Acknowledge the consensus: "I know most people believe [X]. And I understand why — [valid reasons for the consensus]."]

[Line 6 — Blank line]

[Lines 7-8 — "But here's where I disagree:"]

[Lines 9-16 — Your argument in 3-4 numbered points. Each point:
  - Your claim
  - Your evidence (experience, data, logic)
  - Why the consensus gets this wrong]

[Line 17 — Blank line]

[Lines 18-20 — Nuance paragraph: Where you admit the consensus IS right. This prevents the post from feeling like a rant and shows intellectual honesty. 2-3 sentences.]

[Line 21 — Blank line]

[Line 22 — Invitation to debate: "I know many of you disagree. Tell me where my reasoning breaks down."]
```

### Example Post

Unpopular opinion: networking events are a waste of time for most professionals.

I know this goes against everything we're taught about career growth. "Your network is your net worth." "It's not what you know, it's who you know." I've heard it all, and I understand why people believe it — some of the most successful people attribute their success to connections.

But here's where I disagree:

1. Most networking events optimize for breadth, not depth. You collect 30 business cards and remember 2 names. Research from Harvard Business Review shows that weak ties only produce value when there's a specific context for follow-up — which cocktail party small talk rarely provides.

2. The ROI is terrible when measured honestly. A 3-hour event + 1 hour commute + 30 minutes of follow-up emails = 4.5 hours for maybe 1 meaningful connection. That same 4.5 hours spent creating content or doing excellent work visible to your industry would reach thousands.

3. The best professional relationships I've built came from working together, not networking together. Shared projects, open-source contributions, and collaborative content create bonds that "So, what do you do?" never will.

4. There's a survivorship bias problem. People who succeeded through networking tell networking stories. People who succeeded through deep work don't attend networking events to tell you about it.

Now, where I agree with the consensus: targeted, small-group events with a specific purpose (mastermind dinners, workshop cohorts, working retreats) DO produce outsized value. The key difference is shared context and commitment, not just proximity.

I know many of you disagree. Tell me where my reasoning breaks down.

### Why It Works
Unpopular opinions trigger what psychologists call "the need for cognitive closure" — readers who disagree cannot stop themselves from composing a rebuttal in their head, which drives them to comment. The nuance paragraph (admitting where you might be wrong) prevents the audience from dismissing you as unreasonable and actually increases engagement because it makes the position feel more defensible. These posts generate the longest comment threads on LinkedIn, which the algorithm interprets as high-value conversation.

## 9. The Pattern Interrupt

**Take something familiar + give it ONE weird twist**

### When to Use
When a conventional format (case study, listicle, announcement) is losing the audience's attention because they've seen it 100 times. The pattern interrupt works by triggering a double-take — the reader starts to scroll past, recognizes something familiar, then stops because ONE element doesn't fit. Use sparingly: one weird element is surprising, two is confusing.

### The Rule
Change exactly one thing. Keep everything else familiar so the contrast lands.

- Case study over cocktails at a bar
- A musical about cybersecurity
- A job listing posted as a resignation letter
- A pricing page written as a ransom note

Crystal clear on the familiar part. Weird on only the one twist.

### Why It Works
The brain is a pattern-matching machine running on autopilot through a feed. When something matches a familiar pattern and then breaks it, the brain fires a surprise signal that physically stops the scroll. The one-twist rule keeps it understandable — too many changes and the brain labels it "weird and confusing" and moves on.

## Quick Reference: Framework Selection Guide

| Situation | Best Framework | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Research uncovered surprising data | **Contrarian Take** or **Data Reveal** | Numbers + surprise = saves + debate |
| You tested something yourself | **Personal Experiment** | First-hand credibility = trust |
| You have specific metrics/analysis | **Data Reveal** | Data = reference material = saves |
| You have a personal failure/lesson | **Story Arc** | Vulnerability = DM shares |
| You can teach one deep technique | **How-To (Expert)** | Actionable value = saves |
| Something just happened in your industry | **News Analysis** | Timeliness = high impressions |
| You have a clear transformation to show | **Before/After** | Contrast = dwell time |
| You genuinely disagree with consensus | **Unpopular Opinion** | Controversy + substance = longest comment threads |

## Universal Post Rules (Apply to ALL Frameworks)

1. **Hook must work in under 200 characters** — that's all that shows before "see more."
2. **No links in the post body** — always put links in the first comment.
3. **End with a specific question**, not a generic one. "What's your experience with async work?" beats "Thoughts?"
4. **Visual rhythm, not just white space** — blank lines between paragraphs are the minimum. The blocks themselves must vary in length: short line, long paragraph, short line, medium, one word. Zoom out (CMD/CTRL −) until the words blur. The text shapes should look like a varied skyline, not a uniform grid. Uniform block sizes = scroll past. Tom Orbach (500+ posts, Forbes 30 Under 30): "I squint my eyes until I can barely read. If all the blocks look the same size, I rewrite. I only hit Post when it's beautiful."
5. **One idea per post** — if you're covering two topics, write two posts.
6. **Specificity over generality** — "47 hours of meetings" beats "too many meetings."
7. **Be online for the first 60 minutes** — reply to every comment. This is non-negotiable for the algorithm.
8. **3-5 hashtags maximum** — at the end of the post body. Mix 1 broad + 2-3 niche.
9. **Target 1,300-1,800 characters** for text posts (30-45 second read time = optimal dwell).
10. **Never edit in the first hour** — it can reset algorithmic scoring.
