# Hook Formulas — Scroll-Stopping Opening Lines

LinkedIn shows only the first 2 lines before "...see more." The hook must earn the click. Every opening line has one job: make the reader unable to scroll past without expanding.

---

## Stat Hooks

Numbers create instant credibility and trigger the "that can't be right" reflex. The key is a stat that contradicts the reader's assumption.

### 1. "[X]% of [audience] [surprising behavior]. Here's why that's a problem."

**Example:** "73% of marketers say they have a content strategy. Only 11% say it's documented. Here's why that's a problem."

**Why it works:** The gap between the two numbers creates cognitive dissonance. The reader needs to resolve it. Uses the **curiosity gap** — the brain can't rest with an unexplained contradiction.

### 2. "I analyzed [X] [things]. Only [Y]% [expected outcome]."

**Example:** "I analyzed 500 LinkedIn posts from tech founders. Only 3% included a single data point. The ones that did got 4x more engagement."

**Why it works:** Personal research implies effort and authority. The low percentage triggers **surprise** — the reader assumed the number would be higher. The implicit "I did the work so you don't have to" earns attention.

### 3. "[Authority source] found that [surprising stat]. Let me break down what that means."

**Example:** "McKinsey found that 70% of digital transformations fail. Let me break down what that means for your team right now."

**Why it works:** **Borrowed credibility** from a recognized authority, combined with a promise of practical interpretation. The reader thinks: "I've heard the stat, but maybe I don't understand it."

### 4. "[Big number] [things] and counting. But here's what nobody mentions."

**Example:** "4.5 billion people use social media daily. But here's what nobody mentions: 90% of them never post."

**Why it works:** The large number anchors attention (**anchoring bias**), and "what nobody mentions" triggers **exclusivity** — the reader feels they're about to learn insider knowledge.

### 5. "The average [professional] spends [X hours] on [task]. That's [Y days] per year."

**Example:** "The average sales rep spends 5.5 hours per week on data entry. That's 36 full workdays per year — gone."

**Why it works:** Converting a small-sounding number into a shocking annual total triggers **loss aversion**. The reader feels the waste viscerally. The dash and "gone" add emotional weight.

## Contrarian Hooks

Contrarian hooks work because LinkedIn is drowning in consensus. Taking the opposite position is a pattern interrupt — the reader stops because you broke the script they expected.

### 1. "Stop [common advice]. It's not working anymore."

**Example:** "Stop posting every day on LinkedIn. It's not working anymore."

**Why it works:** Directly attacks a behavior the reader is currently doing, triggering **threat response** — "Wait, am I wasting my time?" The reader must click to find out if their effort is misguided.

### 2. "Unpopular opinion: [conventional wisdom] is dead."

**Example:** "Unpopular opinion: the 9-to-5 isn't dead. Remote work is just making bad management visible."

**Why it works:** "Unpopular opinion" is a **social signal** that the author is willing to take a risk. It frames the reader as part of an in-group brave enough to consider uncomfortable ideas. The reversal of a cliche ("9-to-5 is dead") adds a second layer of surprise.

### 3. "Everyone is talking about [trend]. Almost everyone is wrong."

**Example:** "Everyone is talking about AI replacing developers. Almost everyone is wrong about which developers."

**Why it works:** Creates an **us vs. them** dynamic — the reader wants to be in the "right" group. The partial agreement ("everyone is talking about it") validates the reader's awareness before pivoting to challenge their understanding.

### 4. "The worst advice I ever followed: '[common advice].'"

**Example:** "The worst advice I ever followed: 'Fake it till you make it.' It nearly destroyed my credibility."

**Why it works:** Combines **vulnerability** (admitting a mistake) with **pattern interrupt** (attacking advice the reader may have followed). The reader clicks because they need to know: "Did I follow bad advice too?"

### 5. "[Trend] isn't the future. Here's what is."

**Example:** "No-code isn't the future. Low-code with AI copilots is. Here's what changed in the last 6 months."

**Why it works:** Attacks a belief the reader may hold, then immediately promises a replacement. The reader can't just dismiss it — they need to see the alternative. Uses **replacement framing**: taking something away only works if you offer something better.

## Story Hooks

Stories bypass the analytical brain and activate the narrative brain. The reader can't help imagining themselves in the scenario. Story hooks work because they create **emotional identification** before the reader's defenses engage.

### 1. "Last [timeframe], I [specific event]. It changed how I think about [topic]."

**Example:** "Last Tuesday, a junior dev on my team caught a bug I'd missed for 3 weeks. It changed how I think about code reviews."

**Why it works:** Specificity ("last Tuesday," "3 weeks") signals this is real, not hypothetical. **Vulnerability** (admitting a miss) builds trust. The reader clicks because they want the lesson without the pain.

### 2. "I almost [dramatic near-miss]. Here's what saved me."

**Example:** "I almost sent a proposal with the wrong client's name in it. Here's what saved me — and it wasn't proofreading."

**Why it works:** Near-misses trigger **vicarious anxiety** — the reader imagines it happening to them. "Here's what saved me" promises a tool or tactic they can steal. The parenthetical denial ("it wasn't proofreading") deepens the curiosity gap.

### 3. "3 years ago, I was [past state]. Today, [current state]. Here's the turning point."

**Example:** "3 years ago, I was mass-applying to jobs on LinkedIn with zero responses. Today, recruiters message me weekly. Here's the turning point."

**Why it works:** **Transformation narrative** — the most powerful story structure. The reader maps themselves onto the "before" state and wants the bridge to the "after." The specific contrast makes it believable.

### 4. "A [person] asked me: '[surprising question].' My answer surprised us both."

**Example:** "A client asked me: 'Why should I keep paying for SEO if AI is going to replace Google?' My answer surprised us both."

**Why it works:** Dialogue is inherently engaging — the reader "hears" the conversation. The question is one the reader might have themselves. "Surprised us both" signals the answer is non-obvious, which triggers the **curiosity gap**.

### 5. "I made a mistake that cost me [specific consequence]. Here's what I learned."

**Example:** "I made a pricing mistake that cost me a $40K contract. Here's what I learned about anchoring."

**Why it works:** **Loss aversion** — the specific dollar amount makes the pain concrete. The reader clicks for two reasons: to learn the lesson AND to feel the relief of learning it vicariously instead of through their own loss.

## Question Hooks

Questions force the reader's brain to start answering — they can't help it. The best question hooks ask something the reader can't answer confidently, which creates an itch that only your post can scratch.

### 1. "What if everything you know about [topic] is wrong?"

**Example:** "What if everything you know about networking is wrong? (It probably is, if you're still collecting business cards.)"

**Why it works:** Challenges the reader's **identity as a competent professional**. The parenthetical jab makes it personal. Nobody can scroll past a direct challenge to their expertise without checking.

### 2. "Why do [X]% of [audience] fail at [task]?"

**Example:** "Why do 92% of New Year's resolutions fail by February? (Hint: it's not about willpower.)"

**Why it works:** The specific percentage adds credibility, and the question implies the reader might be in the failing group. The hint in parentheses denies the obvious answer, which forces the reader to seek the real one. **Zeigarnik effect** — open questions demand closure.

### 3. "What separates [successful group] from [unsuccessful group]?"

**Example:** "What separates founders who raise Series A from the 90% who don't? I talked to 12 VCs. They all said the same thing."

**Why it works:** **Self-selection** — the reader identifies with one group and wants confirmation, or identifies with the other and wants the fix. The specificity ("12 VCs," "same thing") adds weight.

### 4. "How would you react if [scenario]?"

**Example:** "How would you react if your biggest client called today and said they're leaving? Most people panic. The best do something counterintuitive."

**Why it works:** Forces the reader into a **mental simulation** — they're already imagining the scenario. "Something counterintuitive" promises a non-obvious solution, which is more interesting than the obvious one.

### 5. "Is [commonly accepted thing] actually hurting your [outcome]?"

**Example:** "Is your morning routine actually hurting your productivity? New research says yes — if you do this one thing."

**Why it works:** Attacks a **sunk cost** — the reader has invested time and identity in their morning routine. The suggestion that it might be harmful triggers defensive curiosity: "I need to check if this applies to me."

## Curiosity Gap Hooks

The curiosity gap is the space between what the reader knows and what they want to know. These hooks deliberately widen that gap. The reader's brain treats it like an open browser tab — it demands closure.

### 1. "I spent [X hours/days] [doing research/experiment]. The results surprised me."

**Example:** "I spent 40 hours analyzing the top 100 LinkedIn posts of 2025. The results surprised me — engagement isn't driven by what you think."

**Why it works:** **Effort signaling** — "40 hours" implies the reader is getting a shortcut to hard-won insight. "Surprised me" from someone who did the work implies the findings are genuinely non-obvious. The reader gets free leverage on someone else's effort.

### 2. "There's a reason [big company] just [action]. And it's not what you think."

**Example:** "There's a reason Apple just killed its car project after 10 years and $10B. And it's not what you think."

**Why it works:** **Authority + mystery** — big companies are assumed to act rationally, so the reason must be interesting. "Not what you think" negates the obvious explanation, forcing the reader to consider alternatives they haven't thought of.

### 3. "I discovered something about [topic] that most [professionals] don't know."

**Example:** "I discovered something about LinkedIn's algorithm that most content creators don't know. It explains why some posts die in 30 minutes."

**Why it works:** **Exclusivity** — "most don't know" positions the reader as potentially uninformed, which threatens their professional identity. The specific detail ("die in 30 minutes") adds concreteness that proves the author has real knowledge.

### 4. "The [industry] has a dirty secret. And it's costing you [consequence]."

**Example:** "The SaaS industry has a dirty secret. And it's costing you 30% more than you should be paying."

**Why it works:** "Dirty secret" implies **hidden information being deliberately withheld** — which triggers both curiosity and mild outrage. The specific consequence ("30% more") personalizes the cost. The reader clicks to find out if they're being taken advantage of.

### 5. "I tracked [metric] for [timeframe]. Here's the pattern nobody talks about."

**Example:** "I tracked my LinkedIn impressions for 6 months, posting at different times each day. Here's the pattern nobody talks about."

**Why it works:** **Empirical personal data** is more credible than opinions. The timeframe (6 months) signals commitment. "Pattern nobody talks about" combines exclusivity with the promise of a system — readers love discovering hidden patterns they can exploit.

## Quick Reference: Choosing the Right Hook

| If you have... | Use this hook type |
|---|---|
| A surprising number or stat | Stat Hook |
| A take that goes against the mainstream | Contrarian Hook |
| A personal experience with a lesson | Story Hook |
| A topic where people are confidently wrong | Question Hook |
| Research or analysis with non-obvious findings | Curiosity Gap Hook |

## Rules for All Hooks

1. **Max 2 lines visible before "see more."** If your hook needs 3 lines to land, it's too long.
2. **No filler words.** Cut "I think," "In my opinion," "I wanted to share." Start with the punch.
3. **Be specific.** "73% of marketers" beats "most marketers." "$40K contract" beats "a big deal."
4. **Don't lie.** Exaggerated hooks erode trust fast. Every claim must be defensible in the body.
5. **Match the hook to the body.** A stat hook that leads to a story-based post feels like bait-and-switch.
6. **Test hooks by reading them out loud.** If they don't make YOU want to read more, rewrite.
