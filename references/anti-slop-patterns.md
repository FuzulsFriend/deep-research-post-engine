# Anti-Slop Patterns

A reference of 30+ AI writing patterns that make LinkedIn posts sound machine-generated. Use this during the draft review phase to catch and eliminate slop before it reaches the user.

---

## 1. Opening Line Slop

The first line of a LinkedIn post determines whether anyone reads line two. These openers signal "AI wrote this" instantly.

| # | Slop Pattern | Action |
|---|-------------|--------|
| 1 | "In today's fast-paced world..." | DELETE. Start with a specific fact, question, or story. |
| 2 | "Let's dive in..." | DELETE. Just start the content. The reader doesn't need a runway. |
| 3 | "Here's the thing..." | DELETE. State the thing directly. |
| 4 | "Have you ever wondered..." | DELETE. State the wonder as a fact or bold question. |
| 5 | "In the ever-evolving landscape of..." | DELETE. Name the specific change that happened. |
| 6 | "Picture this:" | DELETE unless followed by a genuinely vivid, specific scene. "Picture this: a conference room" does not count. |
| 7 | "It's no secret that..." | DELETE. If it's no secret, just say it. This phrase adds zero information. |
| 8 | "As we stand at the crossroads of..." | DELETE. Name the actual decision or tension. |
| 9 | "In an era where..." | DELETE. Name the specific moment, event, or shift. |
| 10 | "I'm excited to share..." | DELETE. Just share it. Your excitement is demonstrated by the quality of what follows. |
| 11 | "Buckle up..." | DELETE. If the content is interesting, the reader won't need a seatbelt warning. |
| 12 | "Stop scrolling..." | DELETE. Ironic — this phrase is exactly what makes people scroll faster. |
| 13 | "Unpopular opinion:" | DELETE unless the opinion is genuinely unpopular. Most "unpopular opinions" on LinkedIn are consensus takes disguised as bravery. |
| 14 | "Hot take:" | Same as above. State the take and let the reader decide the temperature. |

## 2. Filler Word Slop

These phrases pad sentences without adding meaning. Every one of them can be removed or shortened without losing information.

| # | Slop Pattern | Action |
|---|-------------|--------|
| 1 | "It's worth noting that..." | DELETE. Just state the thing. The reader decides what's worth noting. |
| 2 | "At the end of the day..." | DELETE. State the conclusion directly. |
| 3 | "It goes without saying..." | DELETE. If it goes without saying, don't say it. |
| 4 | "Needless to say..." | DELETE. Same logic — you're saying it, so apparently it's not needless. |
| 5 | "To be honest..." | DELETE. Implies you're usually not honest. |
| 6 | "The reality is..." | DELETE. Just state the reality. |
| 7 | "What's interesting is..." | DELETE. Let the reader decide what's interesting. Telling them what to find interesting is patronizing. |
| 8 | "It's important to understand that..." | DELETE. Just state the understanding. |
| 9 | "When it comes to..." | REPLACE with the specific subject. "When it comes to hiring" becomes "Hiring..." |
| 10 | "In terms of..." | REPLACE with the specific subject. "In terms of revenue" becomes "Revenue..." |
| 11 | "At this point in time..." | REPLACE with "Now" or delete entirely. |
| 12 | "Due to the fact that..." | REPLACE with "Because". |
| 13 | "In order to..." | REPLACE with "To". |
| 14 | "The question is..." | DELETE. Ask the question directly. |
| 15 | "That being said..." | DELETE. The contrast should be clear from context. If it's not, rewrite the transition. |

## 3. Buzzword Slop

Words that sound impressive but communicate nothing specific. Each one is a missed opportunity to say something concrete.

| # | Buzzword | Replacement |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | "game-changer" | Describe the specific change. "Reduced our deploy time from 4 hours to 12 minutes" is a game-changer you can feel. |
| 2 | "leverage" | "Use". If "use" sounds too simple, the sentence is probably hiding weak content behind a fancy verb. |
| 3 | "unlock" | "Enable", or better — describe what actually becomes possible. |
| 4 | "delve" | "Examine", "look at", or "dig into" if the tone is casual. |
| 5 | "tapestry" | DELETE or use the specific word. "A tapestry of cultures" means nothing. Name the cultures. |
| 6 | "landscape" | Name the specific field, industry, or market. "The AI landscape" becomes "the way companies are deploying AI agents." |
| 7 | "navigate" | Name the specific action. "Navigate uncertainty" becomes "make decisions without complete data." |
| 8 | "paradigm shift" | Describe the specific shift. What was the old way? What's the new way? That's your sentence. |
| 9 | "synergy" | Describe the specific combination and what it produces. |
| 10 | "disruptive" | Describe what it actually disrupts and how. "Disruptive technology" means nothing without the disruption story. |
| 11 | "cutting-edge" | Describe what makes it new. What existed before? What does this do differently? |
| 12 | "best-in-class" | Provide a specific comparison. Best compared to what? By what metric? |
| 13 | "robust" | Describe what makes it strong. "Robust security" becomes "encrypted at rest and in transit with quarterly pen tests." |
| 14 | "holistic" | Describe what aspects are covered. "Holistic approach" becomes "covers hiring, onboarding, and retention." |
| 15 | "ecosystem" | Name the specific system. "The startup ecosystem" becomes "seed-stage founders in Tel Aviv." |
| 16 | "empower" | Describe what people can now do that they couldn't before. |
| 17 | "revolutionize" | Describe the before and after. If the before/after isn't dramatic, it's not a revolution. |
| 18 | "scalable" | Describe the scale. "Scalable solution" becomes "handles 10x traffic without adding servers." |
| 19 | "actionable" | Just make the advice specific enough that the reader can act on it. You won't need the word. |
| 20 | "thought leader" | Never use this to describe yourself or your user. Let the content speak. |

## 4. Structural Slop

Patterns in how a post is organized that signal AI generation, even when individual sentences are clean.

| # | Pattern | Fix |
|---|---------|-----|
| 1 | Generic lists (5/7/10 items without depth) | Fewer items (3-4) with real insight, personal experience, or data per item. A 3-item list where each item has a story beats a 10-item list of surface claims. |
| 2 | Every paragraph is the same length | Vary the rhythm. Short punchy paragraph (1-2 sentences) followed by a longer explanatory one (3-4 sentences). Monotone paragraph length is an AI fingerprint. |
| 3 | No personal experience anywhere | Add "I tested this", "In my experience", "Last week I...", or "A client of mine...". First-person specificity is the hardest thing for AI to fake. |
| 4 | No specific data or numbers | Add real numbers from the research phase. "Significant growth" becomes "grew 340% in 6 months." Vague claims are AI tells. |
| 5 | Ending with "What do you think?" without earning it | Ask a SPECIFIC question that proves you thought about the reader. "What do you think?" is lazy. "Have you tried X and found Y?" gives the reader a foothold to respond. |
| 6 | Starting every sentence with "I" | Vary sentence starters. "I built... I learned... I realized..." reads like a robot journaling. Mix in "The team...", "One thing that surprised me...", "After three failed attempts..." |
| 7 | Using passive voice throughout | Use active voice and name the actor. "Mistakes were made" becomes "We shipped a broken build to production." Passive voice hides the human behind the story. |
| 8 | Every section follows the same formula | Break the pattern. If every point is "Claim + Explanation + Example", drop the example on one point, lead with the example on another. Predictable structure is invisible structure. |
| 9 | Conclusion restates the introduction | The conclusion should go further than the intro — add a new angle, a personal commitment, or a question that extends the thinking. Restating the intro is a book report move. |
| 10 | Listicle with no connective tissue | Add transitions between items that show why the order matters or how items relate. "And this leads to..." or "The opposite problem is..." |

## 5. Detection Rule

**If 3 or more slop patterns are found in a draft, do NOT patch — rewrite from scratch.**

Slop compounds. A post with 3 fixes still reads like AI wrote it and someone edited it. The seams show. The rhythm is off. The reader can feel it even if they can't name it.

A rewrite from the same research brief — using the voice profile as a guide — will be cleaner, faster, and more authentic than a patched draft.

### Severity Levels

- **1-2 patterns found:** Patch in place. Replace or delete the specific phrases. Read the full post aloud after patching to check flow.
- **3-4 patterns found:** Rewrite from scratch using the same research brief and voice profile. Do not reference the sloppy draft.
- **5+ patterns found:** Rewrite from scratch AND review whether the research brief itself contains slop (garbage in, garbage out). Clean the brief first, then rewrite.

### The Read-Aloud Test

After any edit or rewrite, read the post aloud in the user's voice (based on the voice profile). If any sentence makes you pause, stumble, or sounds like it belongs in a press release rather than a LinkedIn post from a real person — rewrite that sentence.
